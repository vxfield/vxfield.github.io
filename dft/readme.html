1: Introduction

This is a tutorial on Density Functional Theory (DTF), a method used in computational physics/chemistry to model many-body quantum-mechanical systems such as atoms, molecules and condensed phases of matter.

In ancient times, people known as alchemists learned to manipulate and transform matter by mixing different elements, heating them up, cooling them down, and changing other environment conditions. The process was extremely laborious and time-consuming, requiring a lot of trial-and-error.

But even today, many things can only be discovered with a trial-and-error approach, executed by experienced researchers in expensive labs.

Density Functional Theory has the potential to drastically cut the cost and time of these discoveries by narrowing-down promising candidates that should be tested in the lab.

For example, DFT can help with:

  1. Predicting the electronic structure of atoms and molecules, such as ground state, chemical potential, orbitals, electronic bands, magnetic properties, charge densities, etc.
  2. Simulating forces between atoms and molecules and their binding energies.
  3. Discovering efficient synthesis of chemical compounds and catalysts, reducing costs and waste in the chemical industry.
  4. Discovering/designing new molecules that interact with other molecules in specific ways, developing new pharmaceutical drugs, creating lighter and stronger materials.

Although knowing the basic principles of electromagnetism, atomic theory, quantum-mechanics, and numerical methods can help, this tutorial assumes you know very little about these topics.

2: Introduction to Quantum Mechanics

2.1: Modelling a simple atom

Let's start with the basics, a single Hydrogen atom. First with some classical principles, then with quantum mechanics.

In the 1900s, Rutherford and colleagues discovered that the atom has positive charges (protons) in the nucleus and negative charges (electrons) orbiting it.


Figure 1: Rutherford model

The electron and proton are have electrical charges, so they will feel the Coulomb force, where ϵ0\epsilon_0ϵ0​ is just a constant, qqq and QQQ are the electrical charges of the electron and the proton, and rrr is the distance between the two.

FCoulomb=14πϵ0q∗Qr2F_{Coulomb} = {1 \over 4 \pi \epsilon_0} {q * Q \over r^2}FCoulomb​=4πϵ0​1​r2q∗Q​

The charge of the electron and the proton have the same magnitude e=1.602×10−19  Coulombse = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \;Coulombse=1.602×10−19Coulombs, but the electron is negative.

So the total force is also negative, creating an attraction:

Felectron−proton=−14πϵ0e2r2F_{electron-proton} = - {1 \over 4 \pi \epsilon_0} {e^2 \over r^2}Felectron−proton​=−4πϵ0​1​r2e2​

A classical particle would normally move in a straight line, so we could think the electron would do the same.
Except it's being pulled by the Coulomb force towards the proton.

But for some "mysterious" reason, instead of it just snapping into the proton like two magnets, it keeps its distance and find an equilibrium, orbiting the proton like the Moon orbits the Earth.

A classical model of this would be the balancing of a centripetal and a centrifugal force.

∣Fcentripetal∣=∣Fcentrifugal∣|F_{centripetal}| = |F_{centrifugal}|∣Fcentripetal​∣=∣Fcentrifugal​∣

The centripetal force is the Coulomb force pulling the electron towards the nucleus.

The centrifugal force is an inertial force of the electron wanting to keep its velocity unchanged and in a straight line. It's given by the mass mmm of the electron, the orbital velocity vvv and the radius rrr.

Fcentrifugal=mv2rF_{centrifugal} = {m v^2 \over r}Fcentrifugal​=rmv2​

Resulting in

14πϵ0e2r2=mev2r{1 \over 4 \pi \epsilon_0} {e^2 \over r^2} = {m_{e} v^2 \over r}4πϵ0​1​r2e2​=rme​v2​

Another way to model this is by the energy of the system.

Etotal=Ekinetic+EpotentialE_{total} = E_{kinetic} + E_{potential}Etotal​=Ekinetic​+Epotential​

The kinetic energy is from the electron's momentum (mass×velocity{mass} \times {velocity}mass×velocity).

Ekinetic=12mev2E_{kinetic} = {1 \over 2} m_e v^2Ekinetic​=21​me​v2

The potential energy is given by the electromagnetic field (result of the Coulomb force).

Epotential=14πϵ0e2rE_{potential} = {1 \over 4 \pi \epsilon_0} {e^2 \over r}Epotential​=4πϵ0​1​re2​

Giving

Etotal=12mev2+14πϵ0e2rE_{total} = {1 \over 2} m_e v^2 + {1 \over 4 \pi \epsilon_0} {e^2 \over r}Etotal​=21​me​v2+4πϵ0​1​re2​

But experiments shown that the Rutherford model was not quite right.

In the 1910s, it was found that the electron can actually orbit the nucleus at different distances, but not any distance.

Bohr proposed that only certain radiuses were allowed, and the electron can leap from one orbit to the next by absorbing or emitting a certain quantum of energy (a photon).


Figure 2: Bohr model

Then, finally in the 1920s, Heisenberg, de Broglie, and Schrodinger formulated a new mathematical model of particles (including protons and electrons) that helped explain why the would not just go into the nucleus and why it's only allowed at certain orbits.

Even more, this new model proposed that particles behave as waves and that the electron is not like a little ball orbiting the nucleus, that we could know for certain where it is at a particular time.

There is a wave (modeled via a mathematical function) that gives the probability of finding the electron at a particular position. And like a water wave can move up and down in a swimming pool, the same goes with the "electron wave".

That lead to the current quantum-mechanical model of the atom, in which there is an "electron cloud" around the nucleus that is more dense at places that we are more likely to find an electron.

This cloud can be spherical, but it can also have some weird shapes, depending on which orbital (and energy) the electron is occupying.


Figure 3: Quantum-mechanical model of the Hydrogen atom

And similar to the classical model, Schrodinger proposed a formulation based on the energy of the system, and that there is a function Ψ\PsiΨ that when applied the energy operator Hˆ\^HHˆ to it would result in the same function Ψ\PsiΨ times the energy EEE of the system.

Hˆ Ψ(x)=E Ψ(x)\^H \, \Psi(x) = E \, \Psi(x)HˆΨ(x)=EΨ(x)

Ψ\PsiΨ is a function that allows one to calculate several things that can be observed in the system, most notably the likelihood of finding a particle, in our case the electron, at a given position xxx (and in time ttt, in case of the time-dependent Schrodinger equation that we will see later).

More specifically, Ψ\PsiΨ is a wave function. It describes how the system evolves with time, and how the energy transfers between kinetic and potential energies.

2.2: Wave functions

For example, a block suspended by a spring will have its energy varying between its kinetic energy (when the block is moving, it has velocity and momentum) and its potential energy (when the block is at either extremes of the motion, it stops and the energy is in a combination of gravitational potential energy and the spring extension/compression energy).


Figure 4: Simple Harmonic Oscillator

This type of system has a periodic behavior and is easier to model it via sine and cosine functions, which are easily observed when you look at phase space of the system and circular "orbit" in the position+velocity space.

A classical wave function has the form of

Ψclassical=A cos⁡(ωt+ϕ)\Psi_{classical} = A \, \cos(\omega t + \phi)Ψclassical​=Acos(ωt+ϕ)

where, AAA is the amplitude of the movement, ω\omegaω is frequency at which the energy moves from kinetic to potential energy, ttt is the moment in time, and ϕ\phiϕ is the phase, which represents the initial conditions (what was the initial position and momentum of the object).

But in Quantum Mechanics, it was hypothesized (de Broglie hypothesis) that every object in the universe has a wave associated to it. But it's not just that the idea that an object can move in a wave-like motion, the idea is that the object itself is a wave. It's hard to makes sense of it because, as far as we know, we can only see objects as definitive, concrete particles and the wave aspect of the objects can only be used to calculate probabilities related to their observable properties.

The de Broglie hypothesis was confirmed thru a variety of experiments, most famously the double-slit experiment in which a single electron was released at a time, and it would go thru two slits (yes, at the same time, it would go thru both!), and then it would hit the detector screen at a random position. But when this experiment is repeated many times, it became clear that the positions were not uniformly random, but instead they respected a probability density that matched what a wave would do if going thru the slits.



Figure 5: Double-slit Experiment

It was also postulated, and proven thru experiments, that that the probability P[x]P[x]P[x] of finding a particle in position xxx is given by the square of the wave function Ψ(x)\Psi(x)Ψ(x). This is known as the Born rule.

P[x]=∣Ψ(x)∣2P[x] = |\Psi(x)|^2P[x]=∣Ψ(x)∣2

Since Ψ(x)\Psi(x)Ψ(x) is a complex function, the ∣z∣2|z|^2∣z∣2 operator is the complex number zzz multiplied by its complex conjugate z∗z^*z∗ (or z‾\overline{z}z)

∣z∣2=z z∗=z z‾z=a+i bz∗=z‾=a−i b|z|^2 = z \, z^* = z \, \overline{z} \\ z = a + i \, b \\ z^* = \overline{z} = a - i \, b∣z∣2=zz∗=zzz=a+ibz∗=z=a−ib

More accurately, the probability of finding the particle between positions ccc and ddd is

P[c≤x≤d]=∫cd∣Ψ(x)∣2 dxP[c \leq x \leq d] = \int_c^d{|\Psi(x)|^2 \, dx}P[c≤x≤d]=∫cd​∣Ψ(x)∣2dx

and the probability of finding the particle somewhere in space must be 100% (this is called normalization)

P[−∞≤x≤∞]=1=∫−∞∞∣Ψ(x)∣2 dxP[-\infty \leq x \leq \infty] = 1 = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{|\Psi(x)|^2 \, dx}P[−∞≤x≤∞]=1=∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x)∣2dx

The de Broglie hypothesis combined with the Born rule, and the fact that the wave function is time-periodic, implies that the general solution for the time-dependent Schrodinger Equation is comprised of complex numbers (several explanations can be found in Karam 2020) and has the form

Ψ(x,t)=∑nAn e−i En tℏ ψEn(x)\Psi(x, t) = \sum_n{A_n \, e^{-i \, E_n \, t \over \hbar} \, \psi_{E_n}(x)}Ψ(x,t)=n∑​An​eℏ−iEn​t​ψEn​​(x)

where xxx is the particle's position, ttt is the moment in time, AnA_nAn​ are complex numbers indicating the amplitude for a given quantized energy level nnn, iii is the imaginary number i=−1i=\sqrt{-1}i=−1​, EnE_nEn​ is the energy at energy level nnn, ℏ\hbarℏ is a constant and ψEn(x)\psi_{E_n}(x)ψEn​​(x) is the time-independent wave function for energy level nnn.

Also, from these results it follows that it's not possible to know both position and momentum of the particle at the same time with arbitrary accuracy (proved by gamma ray experiments).
This is know as the Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

σxσp≥ℏ2\sigma_x \sigma_p \geq {\hbar \over 2}σx​σp​≥2ℏ​

where σx\sigma_xσx​ is the standard deviation of position xxx and σp\sigma_pσp​ is the standard deviation of position ppp.

A particle, such an electron, bound by a potential (such as the Coulomb potential of a proton) behaves similar to that block on the spring.

2.3: Schrodinger Equation

We digressed a bit on the idea of wave function, but it was a concept important to grasp.

Now, let's get back to the Schrodinger Equation we started with.

To recap, there is a function Ψ\PsiΨ that when applied the energy operator Hˆ\^HHˆ to it would result in the same function Ψ\PsiΨ times the energy EEE of the system.

Hˆ Ψ(x)=E Ψ(x)\^H \, \Psi(x) = E \, \Psi(x)HˆΨ(x)=EΨ(x)

Ψ\PsiΨ is a complex-number wave function. It describes how the system evolves with time, one can use it to calculate the probabilities of measuring the values of observable properties of the system.

Hˆ\^HHˆ is the energy operator, known as the Hamiltonian, and it's comprised of a kinetic and a potential energy operator.

Hˆ=Tˆ+Vˆ\^H = \^T + \^VHˆ=Tˆ+Vˆ

Tˆ=−ℏ2m∂2∂x2\^T = {-\hbar \over 2m} {\partial^2 \over \partial x^2 }Tˆ=2m−ℏ​∂x2∂2​

Vˆ=V(x)\^V = V(x)Vˆ=V(x)

Vˆ\^VVˆ is the potential energy operator, which is just a function that returns the potential energy at a given position xxx (and in time, in case of the time-dependent Schrodinger equation).

The (time-independent) Schrodinger Equation is then

[−ℏ2m∂2∂x2+V(x)]Ψ(x)=E Ψ(x)\left[{-\hbar \over 2m} {\partial^2 \over \partial x^2 } + V(x)\right] \Psi(x) = E \, \Psi(x)[2m−ℏ​∂x2∂2​+V(x)]Ψ(x)=EΨ(x)

So, if we want to find the likelihood of finding the position of the electron in the Hydrogen atom, we need to find the wave function Ψ(x)\Psi(x)Ψ(x) for it.

3.3: Born-Oppenheimer Approximation

As we saw before, analytically solving the Schrodinger Equation for more than two particles is pretty much unfeasible, as the problem scales exponentially with the number of particles.

So we need to solve the problem using a numerical method, and we also need to make some approximations otherwise even a numerical method may become too complex to be computed in a reasonable time.

The first approximation we already applied was considering all the particles in the nucleus as a single point charge.
The rationale for that is that the electrons are relatively far from the nucleus to perceived it as single point charge (Gauss Law).

The second approximation we will do, is called the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, in which we consider that the nucleus is not moving relative to all the electrons that are zipping around it,
The rationale for that us that the mass of the nucleus is 4 orders of magnitude larger than of the electron's.
From the electrons perspective, the nucleus is too heavy to move, and it's velocity (and kinetic energy) is practically zero.

That further simplifies our Hamiltonian

Hˆ=−∑i12∇i2⏟Tˆe−∑A12MA∇A2⏟TˆN=0+∑A<BZAZBrA,B⏟VˆNN=constant−∑iAZArA,i⏟VˆNe+∑i<j1ri,j⏟Vˆee\^H = \underbrace{- \sum_{i}{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i}_{\^T_e} \underbrace{- \sum_{A}{1 \over 2 M_A} \nabla^2_A}_{\^T_N=0} \underbrace{+ \sum_{A<B}{Z_A Z_B \over r_{A,B}}}_{\^V_{NN}=\text{constant}} \underbrace{- \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}}}_{\^V_{Ne}} \underbrace{+ \sum_{i<j}{1 \over r_{i,j}}}_{\^V_{ee}}Hˆ=Tˆe​−i∑​21​∇i2​​​TˆN​=0−A∑​2MA​1​∇A2​​​VˆNN​=constant+A<B∑​rA,B​ZA​ZB​​​​VˆNe​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​Vˆee​+i<j∑​ri,j​1​​​

Since the nuclear repulsion is a constant, it only impacts the total energy of the system, but it does not affect the wave function itself.

So we can focus on solving the electronic wave function with its correspinding electronic Hamiltonian

Hˆelec∣ψelec⟩=Eelec∣ψelec⟩\^H_{elec} |\psi_{elec}\rangle = E_{elec} |\psi_{elec}\rangleHˆelec​∣ψelec​⟩=Eelec​∣ψelec​⟩

3.4: Hartree Products

Even with the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, the electronic equation is still hard to solve because:

  1. There are N(N−1)2!N(N-1) \over 2!2!N(N−1)​ interacting pair of electrons that repel each other in Vˆee\^V_{ee}Vˆee​. For a simple H2\text{H}_2H2​ molecule that is only one pair, but for a O2\text{O}_2O2​ molecule that is 56 pairs!
  2. We need to use a numerical method to solve the Schrodinger differential equation. For an O2\text{O}_2O2​ molecule, we have 16 electrons in total, each one with 3 spatial dimensions. For example, if we discretize each dimension with 10 points, we would need to compute 103×(8+8)=104810^{3 \times (8+8)}=10^{48}103×(8+8)=1048 operations. The fastest high-performance computers can do around 101810^{18}1018 operations per second. So we would need 103010^{30}1030 seconds, but the age of the universe is "only" 101710^{17}1017 seconds!

So another assumption we are going to make is to consider that electrons don't interact with each other. This is not a good assumption because electrons do interact with each other, but we will start with this, and later try adjust our Hamiltonian by bringing the electron interaction back.

If we pretend that's the case, then our Hamiltonian gets much simpler because the electron repulsion term Vˆee\^V_{ee}Vˆee​ disappear, and the remaining terms are independent for each electron.

Hˆelec=−∑i12∇i2⏟Tˆe−∑iAZArA,i⏟VˆNe+∑i<j1ri,j⏟Vˆee=0\^H_{elec} = \underbrace{- \sum_{i}{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i}_{\^T_e} \underbrace{- \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}}}_{\^V_{Ne}} \underbrace{+ \sum_{i<j}{1 \over r_{i,j}}}_{\^V_{ee}=0}Hˆelec​=Tˆe​−i∑​21​∇i2​​​VˆNe​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​Vˆee​=0+i<j∑​ri,j​1​​​

Now, instead of solving a single equation with 3×N3 \times N3×N spatial dimensions, we can solve NNN separate equations with only 3 spatial dimensions each, which is much simpler to do.

The spin-orbital χ(x⃗i)\chi(\vec{x}_i)χ(xi​) becomes an eigenfunction of hˆ(i)\^h(i)hˆ(i), with E\mathcal{E}E as the orbital energy.

hˆ(i)=−12∇i2⏟Tˆei−∑iAZArA,i⏟VˆNei\^h(i) = \underbrace{-{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i}_{\^T_{e_i}} \underbrace{- \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}}}_{\^V_{Ne_i}}hˆ(i)=Tˆei​​−21​∇i2​​​VˆNei​​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​

hˆ(i)χ(x⃗i)=Eχ(x⃗i)\^h(i) \chi(\vec{x}_i) = \mathcal{E} \chi(\vec{x}_i)hˆ(i)χ(xi​)=Eχ(xi​)

Our total electronic Hamiltonian Hˆelec\^H_{elec}Hˆelec​ becomes the sum of each individual electron Hamiltonian hˆ(i)\^h(i)hˆ(i)

Hˆelec=∑i=1Nhˆ(i)\^H_{elec} = \sum_{i=1}^N \^h(i)Hˆelec​=i=1∑N​hˆ(i)

And the general electronic wave function Ψelec\Psi_{elec}Ψelec​ is the product of each independent and uncorrelated electron wave function χi(x⃗i)\chi_i(\vec{x}_i)χi​(xi​).

This known as the Hartree Product of the spin-orbitals in the system.

Ψelec({x⃗i})=∏i=1Nχi(x⃗i)\Psi_{elec}(\{\vec{x}_i\}) = \prod_{i=1}^N \chi_i(\vec{x}_i)Ψelec​({xi​})=i=1∏N​χi​(xi​)

3.5: Anti-Symmetry and Slater Determinants

One problem with the Hartree Product is that it does not respect the Anti-Symmetry Principle.

In nature, as observed by multiple experiments, it's impossible to distinguish between two electrons. They all look the same.

The way we modelled the product of the spin-orbitals in the Hartree Product, we tied the wave function χ1\chi_1χ1​ of electron 111 with the position x⃗1\vec{x}_1x1​, and so on for all χi(x⃗i)\chi_i(\vec{x}_i)χi​(xi​).

This is a problem because it assumes that we know that electron 111 is at position x⃗1\vec{x}_1x1​, 222 is at x⃗2\vec{x}_2x2​, etc.
But in reality we can't distinguish them, and it means that electron 111 could be at position x⃗2\vec{x}_2x2​, or at any other x⃗i\vec{x}_ixi​.

The way to correctly model this anti-symmetry principle is to change the sign of the total electronic wave function every time we exchange electrons in our model.

For example, if we start with this wave function and exchange electrons 111 and 222

Ψelec(x⃗1,x⃗2)=χ1(x⃗1)χ2(x⃗2)\Psi_{elec}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \chi_1(\vec{x}_1) \chi_2(\vec{x}_2)Ψelec​(x1​,x2​)=χ1​(x1​)χ2​(x2​)

We need to add a negative sign to the wave function

Ψelec(x⃗2,x⃗1)=−χ1(x⃗2)χ2(x⃗1)\Psi_{elec}(\vec{x}_2, \vec{x}_1) = - \chi_1(\vec{x}_2) \chi_2(\vec{x}_1)Ψelec​(x2​,x1​)=−χ1​(x2​)χ2​(x1​)

So our total electronic wave function needs to have a linear combination of every Hartree Product permutation with the correct signs applied to them.

In our two-electron example, it would look like this (electron 1 could be at either position x⃗1\vec{x}_1x1​ or x⃗2\vec{x}_2x2​)

Ψelec(x⃗1,x⃗2)=12[χ1(x⃗1)χ2(x⃗2)−χ2(x⃗1)χ1(x⃗2)]\Psi_{elec}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = {1 \over \sqrt{2}} \left[ \chi_1(\vec{x}_1) \chi_2(\vec{x}_2) - \chi_2(\vec{x}_1) \chi_1(\vec{x}_2) \right]Ψelec​(x1​,x2​)=2​1​[χ1​(x1​)χ2​(x2​)−χ2​(x1​)χ1​(x2​)]

A feature of this linear combination is that it enforces the Pauli Exclusion Principle, which states that two fermions cannot simultaneously have the same occupy the same quantum state (in our case, two electrons cannot occupy the same orbital at the same time).
If both χ1\chi_1χ1​ and χ2\chi_2χ2​ are the same function, meaning that they are the same spin-orbital, the two terms will cancel and the total electronic wave function will be zero (i.e. zero probability of that happening). The same will happen if the two electrons are in the same position.

To represent each possible permutation of Hartree Product we need N!N!N! terms with the correct signs, which quickly gets insanely large to write it down.
Instead we can use Slater Determinant to represent all these permutations in a compact form.

Ψelec(x⃗1,x⃗2)=12∣χ1(x⃗1)χ2(x⃗1)χ1(x⃗2)χ2(x⃗2)∣\Psi_{elec}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = {1 \over \sqrt{2}} \left| \begin{array}{cc} \chi_1(\vec{x}_1) & \chi_2(\vec{x}_1) \\ \chi_1(\vec{x}_2) & \chi_2(\vec{x}_2) \\ \end{array} \right|Ψelec​(x1​,x2​)=2​1​​χ1​(x1​)χ1​(x2​)​χ2​(x1​)χ2​(x2​)​​

Or for a general case

Ψelec(x⃗1,x⃗2,...,x⃗N)=1N!∣χ1(x⃗1)χ2(x⃗1)...χN(x⃗1)χ1(x⃗2)χ2(x⃗2)...χN(x⃗2)⋮⋮⋱⋮χ1(x⃗N)χ2(x⃗N)...χN(x⃗N)∣\Psi_{elec}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2, ..., \vec{x}_N) = {1 \over \sqrt{N!}} \left| \begin{array}{cccc} \chi_1(\vec{x}_1) & \chi_2(\vec{x}_1) & ... & \chi_N(\vec{x}_1) \\ \chi_1(\vec{x}_2) & \chi_2(\vec{x}_2) & ... & \chi_N(\vec{x}_2) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \chi_1(\vec{x}_N) & \chi_2(\vec{x}_N) & ... & \chi_N(\vec{x}_N) \\ \end{array} \right|Ψelec​(x1​,x2​,...,xN​)=N!​1​​χ1​(x1​)χ1​(x2​)⋮χ1​(xN​)​χ2​(x1​)χ2​(x2​)⋮χ2​(xN​)​......⋱...​χN​(x1​)χN​(x2​)⋮χN​(xN​)​​

The Slater Determinant also enforces the Pauli Exclusion Principle, similar to what we saw before with the linear combination.

⟨ΨA∣ΨB⟩={1 if all {χi} are the same0 otherwise\langle \Psi_A | \Psi_B \rangle = \left\{ \begin{array}{l} 1 \text{ if all } \{\chi_i\} \text{ are the same} \\ 0 \text{ otherwise} \end{array} \right.⟨ΨA​∣ΨB​⟩={1 if all {χi​} are the same0 otherwise​

3.6: Excited States and Excited Determinants

Now that we have the Slater Determinant that account for the linear combination of all permutations of single-electron wave functions we are ready to start solving the electronic Schrodinger Equation, right? Well, no so fast...

We applied the Slater Determinant with the goal of not knowing which electron is which and at which location it is. A unidentifiable electron can be in any orbit.

But how many orbits did we consider? If you check our total wave function we have NNN orbitals.

Ψelec({x⃗i})=∏i=1Nχi(x⃗i)\Psi_{elec}(\{\vec{x}_i\}) = \prod_{i=1}^N \chi_i(\vec{x}_i)Ψelec​({xi​})=i=1∏N​χi​(xi​)

That is alright for an atom/molecule in its ground state, but it's very likely that electrons will get excited and occupy other orbitals too, as we saw in the Bohr model of the atom.

So, for a more accurate estimation, we need to account for a few orbitals above the ground state, although in theory there could be infinite orbitals that can be occupied.

For example, if we have 2 electrons, our ground state wave function will be (using the ket-notation)

∣Ψ0⟩=∣χ1,χ2⟩|\Psi_{0}\rangle = | \chi_1, \chi_2 \rangle∣Ψ0​⟩=∣χ1​,χ2​⟩

But if electron 111 gets excited to orbital 444, it will look like this

∣Ψ14⟩=∣χ4,χ2⟩|\Psi_1^4\rangle = | \chi_4, \chi_2 \rangle∣Ψ14​⟩=∣χ4​,χ2​⟩

A general ground and singly-excited state (electron aaa excited to orbital rrr) looks like

∣Ψ0⟩=∣χ1,χ2,...,χa,...,χN⟩∣Ψar⟩=∣χ1,χ2,...,χr,...,χN⟩|\Psi_0\rangle = | \chi_1, \chi_2, ..., \chi_a, ..., \chi_N \rangle \\ |\Psi_a^r\rangle = | \chi_1, \chi_2, ..., \chi_r, ..., \chi_N \rangle∣Ψ0​⟩=∣χ1​,χ2​,...,χa​,...,χN​⟩∣Ψar​⟩=∣χ1​,χ2​,...,χr​,...,χN​⟩

Since we could have multiple electron excitations, as each one can potentially jump to a different orbital, so our total electronic wave function can be in a M-fold\text{M-fold}M-fold excited state with NNN electrons jumping to potentially KKK orbitals.

∣Ψa b ... gMr s ... eM⟩|\Psi_{a\,b\,...\,g_M}^{r\,s\,...\,e_M}\rangle∣Ψab...gM​rs...eM​​⟩

We can also split the total wave function by a linear combination of the different levels of energy states.

∣Ψ⟩=C0∣Ψ0⟩⏟ground state+∑arCar∣Ψar⟩⏟single excited states+∑a<b, r<sCa br s∣Ψa br s⟩⏟double excited states+...|\Psi\rangle = \underbrace{C_0 |\Psi_0\rangle}_{\text{ground state}} + \underbrace{\sum_{ar} C_a^r |\Psi_a^r\rangle}_{\text{single excited states}} + \underbrace{\sum_{a<b,\,r<s} C_{a\,b}^{r\,s} |\Psi_{a\,b}^{r\,s}\rangle}_{\text{double excited states}} + ...∣Ψ⟩=ground stateC0​∣Ψ0​⟩​​+single excited statesar∑​Car​∣Ψar​⟩​​+double excited statesa<b,r<s∑​Cabrs​∣Ψabrs​⟩​​+...

In the ground state, we know that all NNN electrons are in the bottom NNN orbitals, so we have NNN orbital wave functions in total to solve.

As we allow excited states, the number of orbital wave functions grows exponentially at N2MN^{2M}N2M for NNN electrons and MMM excitations (the factor of 222 is because of to spin orbitals for each energy level).

The Hartree-Fock method focus on solving only the ground state determinant, and then other methods can be used to solve the excited determinants known as Configuration Interactions. They come as combinations of Singles, Doubles, Triples, etc., abbreviated as CIS, CISD, CISDT, etc. up to a Full CI.

3.7: Restricted Determinants

So far we have been treating each spin-orbital as a separate wave function χi\chi_iχi​, but in the most common cases every pair of χi\chi_iχi​ shares the same spatial orbital, since two electrons can share the same spatial orbitals by having opposite α\alphaα and β\betaβ spins.

We can then use what's called a restricted determinant for those pairs of electrons.
The restricted determinants are most used when solving systems with even-numbered electrons and/or closed shells, such as organic molecules.

In that case, it's convenient to use the following notation:

∣χiχi+1⟩=∣ψiψ‾i⟩| \chi_{i} \chi_{i+1}\rangle = | \psi_i \overline\psi_i \rangle∣χi​χi+1​⟩=∣ψi​ψ​i​⟩

In which ψi\psi_iψi​ is a short-hand for ψi∣α⟩\psi_i |\alpha\rangleψi​∣α⟩ and ψ‾i\overline\psi_iψ​i​ a short-hand for ψi∣β⟩\psi_i |\beta\rangleψi​∣β⟩, both for a ψi\psi_iψi​ spatial wave function.

This way, the ground wave function can be represented as:

∣Ψ0⟩=∣ψ1ψ‾1ψ2ψ‾2...ψN/2ψ‾N/2⟩| \Psi_0 \rangle = | \psi_1 \overline\psi_1 \psi_2 \overline\psi_2 ... \psi_{N/2} \overline\psi_{N/2} \rangle∣Ψ0​⟩=∣ψ1​ψ​1​ψ2​ψ​2​...ψN/2​ψ​N/2​⟩

We could also have unrestricted determinants in which a α,β\alpha, \betaα,β pair of electrons can have different spatial orbitals.
This useful for open shell systems such as metals and catalysts, but more complex than the restricted determinants.

There are 3 variants of the Hartree-Fock method:

Variant Acronym Variant Applications

UHF Unrestricted Hartree-Fock Open-shell or odd-numbered systems

3.8: One-Electron Integrals

Now back to our total electronic Hamiltonian we had

Hˆelec=−∑i12∇i2⏟Tˆe−∑iAZArA,i⏟VˆNe+∑i<j1ri,j⏟Vˆee\^H_{elec} = \underbrace{- \sum_{i}{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i}_{\^T_e} \underbrace{- \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}}}_{\^V_{Ne}} \underbrace{+ \sum_{i<j}{1 \over r_{i,j}}}_{\^V_{ee}}Hˆelec​=Tˆe​−i∑​21​∇i2​​​VˆNe​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​Vˆee​+i<j∑​ri,j​1​​​

The first two terms (Tˆe\^T_eTˆe​ and VˆNe\^V_{Ne}VˆNe​) are only dependent on an individual one-electron kinetic energy and potential energy due to the nuclear attractions. The second term (Vˆee\^V_{ee}Vˆee​) is a pair-wise sum of potential energies due to each electron repulsion against every other electron.

So what we can do is group the Hamiltonian terms into two operators, Oˆ1\^O_{1}Oˆ1​ for the one-electron terms and Oˆ2\^O_{2}Oˆ2​ for the two-electron term.

Hˆelec=−∑i12∇i2−∑iAZArA,i⏟Oˆ1+∑i<j1ri,j⏟Oˆ2\^H_{elec} = \underbrace{ - \sum_{i}{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i - \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}} }_{\^O_{1}} \underbrace{ + \sum_{i<j}{1 \over r_{i,j}} }_{\^O_{2}}Hˆelec​=Oˆ1​−i∑​21​∇i2​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​Oˆ2​+i<j∑​ri,j​1​​​

Hˆelec=∑iOˆ1(i)+∑i<jOˆ2(ij)\^H_{elec} = \sum_{i} \^O_1^{(i)} + \sum_{i<j} \^O_2^{(ij)}Hˆelec​=i∑​Oˆ1(i)​+i<j∑​Oˆ2(ij)​

The expected ground energy of the system is computed by integration of the Hamiltonian sandwiched between the complex conjugate of the wave function and the regular wave function (remember that xxx contains the three spatial coordinates and one spin coordinates).

E=∫dx  Ψ0∗(x) Hˆ Ψ0(x)E = \int dx \; \Psi_0^*(x) \, \^H \, \Psi_0(x)E=∫dxΨ0∗​(x)HˆΨ0​(x)

Or in Dirac notation

E=⟨Ψ0∣Hˆ∣Ψ0⟩E = \langle \Psi_0 | \^H | \Psi_0 \rangleE=⟨Ψ0​∣Hˆ∣Ψ0​⟩

The total energy of the system can also be split in two parts, for each Oˆi\^O_iOˆi​ operator.

E=∑i⟨Ψ0∣Oˆ1(i)∣Ψ0⟩⏟E1+∑i<j⟨Ψ0∣Oˆ2(ij)∣Ψ0⟩⏟E2E = \underbrace{ \sum_{i} \langle \Psi_0 | \^O_1^{(i)} | \Psi_0 \rangle }_{E_1} + \underbrace{ \sum_{i<j} \langle \Psi_0 | \^O_2^{(ij)} | \Psi_0 \rangle }_{E_2}E=E1​i∑​⟨Ψ0​∣Oˆ1(i)​∣Ψ0​⟩​​+E2​i<j∑​⟨Ψ0​∣Oˆ2(ij)​∣Ψ0​⟩​​

The one-electron operator Oˆ1\^O_{1}Oˆ1​ is just a sum of the one-electron Hamiltonian hˆi\^h_ihˆi​ that we saw before when we assumed there was no electron-electron interaction.
hˆi\^h_ihˆi​ is known as the Core Hamiltonian, and it is the easier to compute.

Oˆ1(i)=hˆi=−12∇i2−∑iAZArA,i\^O_1^{(i)} = \^h_i = -{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i - \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}}Oˆ1(i)​=hˆi​=−21​∇i2​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​

The single one-electron energy, known as the core energy is

hi=⟨χi∣hˆi∣χi⟩h_i = \langle \chi_i | \^h_i | \chi_i \ranglehi​=⟨χi​∣hˆi​∣χi​⟩

hi=∫dxi  χi∗(xi) hˆi χi(xi)h_i = \int dx_i \; \chi_i^*(x_i) \, \^h_i \, \chi_i(x_i)hi​=∫dxi​χi∗​(xi​)hˆi​χi​(xi​)

This allow us to calculate the individual energy contribution of each electron separately. Instead of having to do one integration with 4N4N4N dimensions, we can do NNN integrations with 444 dimensions, a much simpler process.

3.9: Two-electron Integrals

Now it comes the hard part! Solving the many-body Schrodinger Equation for when we have electron-electron interactions (in our Oˆ2\^O_2Oˆ2​ operator).

Hˆelec=−∑i12∇i2−∑iAZArA,i⏟Oˆ1+∑i<j1ri,j⏟Oˆ2\^H_{elec} = \underbrace{ - \sum_{i}{1 \over 2} \nabla^2_i - \sum_{iA}{Z_A \over r_{A,i}} }_{\^O_{1}} \underbrace{ + \sum_{i<j}{1 \over r_{i,j}} }_{\^O_{2}}Hˆelec​=Oˆ1​−i∑​21​∇i2​−iA∑​rA,i​ZA​​​​Oˆ2​+i<j∑​ri,j​1​​​

The Oˆ2\^O_2Oˆ2​ operator is the total potential energy due to electron-electron repulsions.

Oˆ2=Vˆee=1ri,j\^O_{2} = \^V_{ee} = {1 \over r_{i,j}}Oˆ2​=Vˆee​=ri,j​1​

The total expectation value for the two-electron energies is

For a given pair of electrons expectation value comes to be the following integrals (after some algebra). Note the exchange of the spin-orbitals χi{\color{blue} \chi_i}χi​ and χj{\color{red} \chi_j}χj​ on the second integral.

v(i,j)=∬dxi dxj  χi∗(xi) χj∗(xj)1ri,jχi(xi) χj(xj)−∬dxi dxj  χi∗(xi) χj∗(xj)1ri,jχj(xi) χi(xj)\begin{align*} v(i,j) =& \iint dx_i \, dx_j \; {\color{blue} \chi_i^*}({\color{blue} x_i}) \, {\color{red}\chi_j^*}({\color{red} x_j}) {1 \over r_{i,j}} {\color{blue} \chi_i}({\color{blue} x_i}) \, {\color{red}\chi_j}({\color{red} x_j}) \\ -& \iint dx_i \, dx_j \; {\color{blue} \chi_i^*}({\color{blue} x_i}) \, {\color{red}\chi_j^*}({\color{red} x_j}) {1 \over r_{i,j}} {\color{red} \chi_j}({\color{blue} x_i}) \, {\color{blue}\chi_i}({\color{red} x_j}) \end{align*}v(i,j)=−​∬dxi​dxj​χi∗​(xi​)χj∗​(xj​)ri,j​1​χi​(xi​)χj​(xj​)∬dxi​dxj​χi∗​(xi​)χj∗​(xj​)ri,j​1​χj​(xi​)χi​(xj​)​

Or in Dirac notation as

v(i,j)=⟨ij∣1ri,j∣ij⟩−⟨ij∣1ri,j∣ji⟩\begin{align*} v(i,j) &= \left \langle {\color{blue} i} {\color{red} j} \left | {1 \over r_{i,j}} \right | {\color{blue} i} {\color{red} j} \right \rangle - \left \langle {\color{blue} i} {\color{red} j} \left | {1 \over r_{i,j}} \right | {\color{red} j} {\color{blue} i} \right \rangle \end{align*}v(i,j)​=⟨ij​ri,j​1​​ij⟩−⟨ij​ri,j​1​​ji⟩​

Sometimes shorthanded as

v(i,j)=⟨ij∣∣ij⟩v(i,j) = \left \langle {\color{blue} i} {\color{red} j} || {\color{blue} i} {\color{red} j} \right \ranglev(i,j)=⟨ij∣∣ij⟩
